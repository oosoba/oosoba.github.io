Agent-based Models (ABMs) are valuable tools for policy analysis. ABMs help analysts explore the emergent consequences of regulatory and policy interventions in multi-agent decision-making settings. But the validity of inferences drawn from ABM explorations depends on the quality of the ABM agents' behavioral models. Prior approaches for specifying behaviors have limitations. This paper examines the value of reinforcement learning (RL) models as adaptive, high-performing, and behaviorally-valid models of agent decision-making in ABMs. We discuss the value of RL for modeling agents' utility-maximizing behaviors in policy-relevant ABMs. We address the problem of adapting RL algorithms to handle multi-agency in games by adapting and extending methods from recent literature.
